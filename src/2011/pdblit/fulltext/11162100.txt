Cloning and sequencing of a gene from the archaeon Pyrococcusfuriosus with high homology to a gene encoding phosphoenolpyruvate synthetase from Escherichia coli

SUMMARY A gene from the hyperthermophilic archaeon Pyrococcusfuriosus, strain Vcl (DSM 3638), contains an 817-aminoacid open reading frame which shows 42% identity to the phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) synthetase of Escherichia coli. This putative P. furiosus P E P synthetase is slightly larger than the E. coli enzyme, the region between residues 58 and 89 being absent from the latter.

INTRODUCTION
Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) synthetase (pyruvate, water dikinase; EC 2.7.9.2) catalyses the ATP-dependent conversion of pyruvate to phosphoenolpyruvate. This reaction is the first committed step of gluconeogenesis in some bacteria and archaea. P E P synthetase activity has been demonstrated in the archaea Methanobacterium thermoautotrophicum (Eyzaguirre et al., 1982) and Pyrococcus furiosus (Sch~ifer and Sch6nheit, 1993), as well as in the Enterobacteriaceae (Cooper and Kornberg, 1974). The ppsA gene encoding P E P synthetase has been cloned and sequenced from Escherichia coli (Niersbach et al., 1992). (a) Nucleotide sequence of the ppsA gene The archaeon P.furiosus (Pf) grows optimally at 100°C (Fiala and Stetter, 1986). Partial purification of P / phosphoglycerate kinase from strain Vcl (DSM 3638) cells gave rise to two major bands by SDS-PAGE analysis (unpublished results). After blotting of the gel onto a Problott T M electroblotting membrane (Applied Biosystems), 16 aa from the N terminus of one of these bands were determined by gas-phase sequencing to be A Y R F I K F E E L S K N D V . Searches of the available SwissProt, GenBank and E M B L databases using the FASTA and TFASTA programmes (Pearson and Lipman, 1987) revealed no significant similarity to any protein. Degenerate oligos designed from this N-terminal sequence were used to screen a genomic library of P f in LambdaGEM®-i 1 and led to the isolation of eight posi-

102 tive clones. One of the positive )v clones (PF 1 ) was analysed on a Southern blot with the degenerate oligo probe used to screen the library. This led to the isolation of a 3.3-kb HindIII fragment from the positive )v clone PF1. This 3.3-kb HindIII fragment was subcloned into the plasmid vector pBluescript®II (KS +) to give the plasmid pF19H4. The 1.9-kb sequence from one end of this 3.3-kb fragment was determined and analysis revealed an incomplete ORF encoding 567 aa of the N-terminal region of a protein. A 2.9-kb EcoRI fragment, in the original PF1 ?v isolate, was found to overlap the 3.3-kb HindIII insert of pF19H4. This EcoRI fragment was also cloned into the plasmid pBluescript®II(KS +) and subsequent sequencing of part of this fragment provided the remainder of the ORF. The two overlapping 3.3-kb HindIII and 2.9-kb EcoRI fragments contained a 2454-bp ORF, encoding a 817-aa protein with a deduced M r of 90484. The sequenced region is shown in Fig. 1. The start codon, ATG, was deduced to be located at nt 144-146 from comparison with the determined N-terminal aa sequence. The N-terminal Met is absent from the determined protein N-terminal sequence, a feature which has been observed previously in the archaea (Eggen et al., 1993). Analysis of the sequence for promoter regions indicated a possible site at nt 87-92 (TTTAAA) which is identical to the consensus archaeal promoter sequence (Dalgaard and Garrett, 1993). Downstream of the stop codon there is a thymidine-rich region typical of many transcriptional terminator regions in the archaea (Dalgaard and Garrett, 1993). The G + C content of the gene is 48.3%, which is higher than that of the entire genome which has been found to be 38% (Fiala and Stetter, 1986). The gene has a similar codon bias to that found in the DNA polymerase gene from the same organism l Uemori et al., 19931, in particular using only the codons AGA and AGG for arginine.

(b) Sequencecomparisons
A search of sequences in the GenBank and EMBL databases with the determined translated gene sequence using the TFASTA programme revealed a striking similarity to E. coli P E P synthetase and a number of pyruvate orthophosphate dikinases (PPDK). Alignment of the Pfaa sequence with E. coli P E P synthetase indicated 42% identity and 63% similarity between the two aa sequences. A lower sequence identity was observed between the Pf translated sequence and maize, Flaveria trinervia and Bacteroides symbiosus P P D K s (31, 31 and 30% respectively; Fig. 2).
